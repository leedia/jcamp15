Downtown East

Aman Gul and Zytoona Hassan, an Afghani couple living in Minneapolis, ride the train by necessity.

The couple sits side by side on the Green Line, heading for Downtown East. Gul, who is blind, and Hassan, who does not have a driver's license, do not have another transportation option.

Gul says they began riding the Green Line "when it started."

They have taken the Green Line to work, to the airport, and everyplace else. "Wherever we want to go, we use the train," Hassan says.

"It's comfortable, more than the bus," Gul adds.

Westgate

Madeline Carras, an etymology intern at the University of Minnesota, rides the train because she has no other travel option.

Carras, staying in Minneapolis for the first time this summer, relies on public transportation to commute.

"For the most part, it has been a pretty safe and easy ride," she says. "It's quick."

She has more of an issue with the bus. "It was ten minutes late this morning," she says.

Snelling Ave

Natasha James, a single mother of two, rides the train with her daughters, Ant'Twanet and Vodra, because it’s convenient.

"It’s easier to bring my daughters with me," says James, who has a seizures that limit her mobility.

She hushes Ant'Twanet, who has started climbing up her seat, by showing her daughter a new game on her phone.

Before the Green Line opened, James says, she rode the bus. "The chaos on the bus caused me to stress out faster,” she says. “It could sometimes trigger a seizure."

In contrast, on the train, "it's a lot easier to calm myself down and prevent myself from going into a seizure or recover."

James uses the train frequently to go to appointments and visit her family downtown St. Paul.

Western Ave

Chris Mertz and Katie Fabrizio, working at the St. Joseph Hospital, ride the train to lunch.

"It's much more convenient just to jump on the train and be dropped right off in front of the lunch places. There's not much wait," Mertz says.

Before the Green Line opened, Mertz and Fabrizio would always eat at the same places. "We always ate right near the hospital," Mertz explains.

"Actually, this is the first day we've done this," Mertz says. "We were just talking about how nice it was to go this far from the office and not have it be a time problem."

Both Mertz and Fabrizio say they would like to see more trains in the Twin Cities. "I would be willing to pay for it. Especially coming from outside the city, it would be nice to drop off your car and ride the train the rest of the way," Mertz says.

Union Depot

Dave Rogers, a Metro Transit engineer, drives the train as his job.

Rogers, the second-most senior operator in the system and the union steward, has driven the train for fourteen years.

"It's a little bit more complicated than you might think," he says. “It's harder than driving a car. You have to maintain exact speeds."

"With the rail, I know the initial expenditures are huge," Rogers says, "but after that, the price per passenger is much lower than it is on other forms of transportation. I think that it creates a pleasing environment for the city. This whole area is growing in leaps and bounds right now, so I think it's good for the economy. I think it's money wisely spent."
